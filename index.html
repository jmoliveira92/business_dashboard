Introduction

This project is a result of my learning process as a Data/Business Analyst from the last year also and the knowledge I have acquired in businesses during the last 7 years.

The project is a real-case and aims to assist a new company who needs to understand the photovoltaic industry and identifying their strenghts and weaknesses. We must be able to operate and assess our performance in a timely and efficient manner. By analyzing our operations, we can identify areas where we excel and where we may need improvement, allowing us to adjust our strategies and investments accordingly. The ultimate goal is to provide actionable insights that will enable the company to make informed decisions and drive the business towards success.

The Problem - Clarification Questions

The Board of Direction (= company’s administration) needs a solution that allows them to track their business and understand some insights. To accomplish this task, I must to build a business/operational dashboard that allows:

  • Know the business performance (detail costs, revenue, profits, etc)
  • Be possible to analyze each project in detail.
  • Understand operatively where we are competitive and where we are not.

The start point of my journey: the company only have a a “time_logging app” (to register the workers time) and a simple accounting table.

The previous step of my work was the creation and implementation of automated strategic data gathering procedures ir order to create a efficient Data Model. However, due to their length, these procedures will not be detailed in this document.

Concepts & Methodology

There are some concepts associated to the industry (i.e. construction) and others related to conception’s model.

  1. Types of projects (3): Residential, Industrial and Utility Scale.

 

  1. Measure Revenue & % Ejecution

Since any project has a “Nº of Budget Hours” (means, the working hours that we must invest into the project), the Administration require the REVENUE to be measure in a daily basis (“daily_revenue”), in accordance to the “Daily Worked Hours”. To understand this concept, it is important to distinguish between two (2) differente ways of measuring revenue:

  1. Residencial Projects: revenue is measured in a way that exceeding the 'budget hours' results in a loss, OR if the team finishes early, it results in a profit (of course, we must add remaining costs, such as travel costs and some material invoices).
  2. Industrial and Utility Scale Projects: these types of projects can last for weeks or even months. To measure revenue, a process must be created based on tasks performed and the time taken to complete each task, varying with the number of workers involved (which may change over the course of days). I like to think of it like a 'Loading bar/Progress bar', similar to when you are charging a battery. For instance, if a utility scale PV system has 100 'tables' (each with 60 PV modules), we don't assemble each table from start to finish, as we need to expedite the mounting process. Initially, we mount the 'pile driving' of all tables. Next, we assemble all the frames, then the crossbeams, followed by the straps, and so on. Therefore, using this methodology, we can measure the '% Theoretical Progress' vs. '% Actual Progress' and the 'Theoretical Daily Revenue (€)' vs. 'Actual Daily Revenue (€)' for each project (I won't detail this spreadsheet’s procedure in this document).

 

  1. Calculate and impute Travel Costs

The company owns several vehicles. Invoices for gas and renting from the Leasing Company for these vehicles are billed on a monthly basis. You can easily calculate the 'Daily Renting Amount' for each vehicle. However, the challenge arises with 'GAS' purchased through the Leasing Gas Card, as it doesn't specify which vehicle it was used for. The objective is to allocate each cost to a specific project on a daily basis. Therefore, it's not feasible to allocate the Leasing Card cost to a particular vehicle. However, an estimation of the distance traveled by each vehicle can be made daily (using GPS trackers, for instance), along with the fuel consumption. Due to these reasons, the administration decided to install GPS trackers to monitor the vehicles' distance traveled and cost, and to 'exclude' (from the 'Operational Accounting') the invoices from the Leasing Company. The vehicle's cost is calculated as = renting cost/per day + cost of kilometers driven. For this purpose, a separate sheet has been created to import GPS distance and multiply it by the gasoline price (always higher to allow for a margin) and the consumption of each vehicle (roughly 13L/100 km). Consequently, the costs of this specific supplier (Leasing Company) must be 'excluded' from our Accounting sheet.

 

  1. Track and Allocate Specific Operational Costs

To monitor all specific operational costs of a project, I utilized a “unique key” called “ProjectCode”. This enables to link all specific costs, such as the worker cost time, invoices, travels, etc. For instance:

  1. Recording work entries in the 'fLabor_force' (time worked by each employee in a day) requires a ProjectCode (achieved by simply joining/merging the 'Work_Schedule_Calendar' with the 'fLabor_force' using the key 'emp_id').
  2. Invoices in the 'fAccounting' table must be associated with a ProjectCode.
  3. Each 'travel' (the mode of transportation used by workers to reach the project location) should be identified by a ProjectCode.

 

  1.                     Track and Allocate Non-Specific Operational Costs

What happens when there is an invoice (e.g., 'tools') that we can't attribute to a specific project? In cases involving operational costs not linked to any particular project, I opted to use the ProjectCode labeled as 'G001.' Then, we need to calculate the 'effort ratio' (=time) allocated to each project every month and distribute the Non-Specific Costs (‘G001’) proportionally among projects. This approach enables us to determine the 'actual costs' for any project.

 

  1.                     A note about Workers Time Cost (”Cost Worked Hours”)

It is crucial to calculate the net cost for each worker, i.e., the company's expense. The cost per hour per worker is dynamic and can vary each month. There are some important details to mention:

  1.            Every employee has a "job_category," which means that each employee has an annual salary determined by the job category.
  2.            Each time an employee goes to work, they receive 5€/day as assistance allowance (as per the Company-Union Agreement).
  3.            Some employees may receive a "Team Bonus" for assuming the responsibility of managing the team on-site and reporting to the operations department. The value varies each month based on certain performance parameters.
  4.            Additionally, some teams receive a "Productivity bonus" based on the outstanding work they have delivered in a specific Project. The total bonus is a sum of all the project’s bonus and it can varies monthly (a team leader can receive both “Productivity Bonus” and “Team Bonus” in the same month).
  5.            The salary is subject to a social security tax of 37%. This means that to pay 100€ to the employee, the company's expense is 137.00€ (excluding "assistance").
  6.             The Company-Union Agreement stipulates that each worker SHOULD work no more than 1760 hours per year. While this value is not obligatory, it is advisable not to overload the workers.

 

  1.                     Common indicators

On this industry, there are common indicators that should be tracked to quickly infer about future projects. These indicators include:

  1. Price per installed capacity (€/kWp) for both sales and internal cost. This indicator enables easy price feedback if a client requests a budget.
  2. Price per Module (€/module): similar to the previous one, but based on the number of panels.

 

Data Model

Based on the methodology presented in the previous chapter, the Data Model have the structure presented below. We must notice that there is several “Fact Tables”, nonetheless, the relationships in the Data Model are aiming to respect the “Star Schema” in order to maintaince the reliability of the model.

  • Dimensional Tables: 
  • dCalendar (full calendar included the Company-Union Agreement holidays)
  • dLocation/Geography
  • dProjectCode
  • dEmployee
  • dVendor_ID
  • Fact Tables: 
  • fAccounting
  • fLabor_time (shows the time worked per day per employee. Also merged the “WorkSchedule Calendar” that shows where each employee was on that day = using the ProjectCode unique key)
  • fRevenue_daily
  • fSalaries (backoffice)
  • fTravels_daily

The main dashboard visualization presents the comprehensive overview of the business, with greater emphasis to the operative aspect. Here we can observe:

  • The number of installed projects (both in terms of potency and quantity).
  • A visual depicting revenue, operative costs, and operative margin.
  • A succinct overview of the revenue's evolution over time. By clicking on a specific month, you can Drill Through to view specific revenue details.

Note: the Board chose to disregard the main structural costs (e.g. backoffice salaries) as the project's objective was to achieve operational efficiency and subsequent scalability. Therefore, I am not presenting the 'overall net margin' of the business.

Conclusions 

 

Attention: More data would be necessary to confirm these assumptions.

Comparing the different types of projects and their performance, it is possible to draw some preliminary deductions:

 

  • The most profitable projects were 'industrial' projects (ranging from 50-150 kWp) and 'Utility Scale' projects with less than 500 kWp (0.5 MWp). The indicator 'AVG €/Wp Gross Margin' reveals that industrial projects were approximately 3-4 times more profitable than residential and utility projects. They also generated significantly higher daily profits.

 

  • The least successful project (with the greatest loss) was also an industrial project. The installation faced many rainy days, causing delays and leading to high costs due to inactive personnel and travel expenses. The key takeaway is that the company should enhance project planning and prioritize industrial projects that are 'close' to the headquarters (in the same state). Alternatively, contractual safeguards could be implemented to mitigate such occurrences.

 

  • The second least successful project was a 'Utility' project with a capacity of 3 MWp. Utility projects with several megawatts are highly competitive. Generally, multiple companies are simultaneously constructing on the site, competing for the next available 'field' until the project is completed. It is reasonable to assume that in such projects, labor teams can be 'concentrated' to work faster and reduce costs. However, it's also true that the likelihood of unforeseen events (like rainy days or logistical delays) is higher. The sole project conducted under these conditions resulted in a loss. Therefore, it's conceivable that smaller projects (e.g., small utility projects with 1 MWp), where the company is the sole entity working in the field and meticulous planning is ensured, could yield better margins.
  • It's interesting to observe in the “Operative margin” visualization on the “Revenue” page that, in a given month, you might have generated more revenue, but not necessarily higher margins.

 

  • Regarding Residential projects, they yield less revenue; however, they are an essential source of “consistent revenue” and can be completed in just one day. The data indicates that residential projects could serve as a secure long-term bet.
  • It's noticeable that the number of sold (or initiated) projects has been declining over the last two (2) months. This has caused a decrease in revenue and in operative costs (since the workers are inactive).

It's important to mention that more data is needed to confirm the previous assumptions.
